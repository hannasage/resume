# Restructuring American Soccer: A Business Analysis Brief
**Version 0.3. July 20, 2026**
**Prepared as cornerstone context for the "legitimate footballing nation by ~2056" mission**

---

## 0. Executive Summary

The United States operates the only major footballing economy in the world where the professional game is structured primarily as a financial asset class rather than a sporting pyramid. Entry to the top divisions is purchased ($500M for MLS San Diego in 2023; $110M for NWSL Denver in 2025). Youth development is largely funded by parents, at $8,000 to $15,000 or more per year at the elite level. And until 2025, no sanctioned professional league had ever committed to promotion and relegation.

Three structural facts define the moment:

1. **The USL is already running the experiment.** In 2025, USL owners voted by supermajority to adopt promotion/relegation across a three-tier system topped by a new Division I league, "USL Premier," launching 2028. This is the live proof-of-concept for an open American pyramid, and its results will shape everything else.
2. **The 2026 World Cup result was better than the easy pessimism about it, and it still proves the point.** The USMNT *did* reach the Round of 16: it won Group D, beat Bosnia and Herzegovina 2-0 in the new Round of 32 (first knockout win since 2002), then lost 4-1 to Belgium in Seattle on July 6. That is the fifth Round-of-16 exit in six attempts since 1994. The ceiling is intact. The sharper way to say it: even at home, with its best-ever generation and record goal tally, the US could not break the Ro16 ceiling. The next North American hosting opportunity plausibly lands in the 2050s (2030 is Spain/Portugal/Morocco; 2034 is Saudi Arabia), which fits a 30-year horizon.
3. **England is a cautionary tale with one great lesson, not a clean model.** The Premier League's 1992 breakaway was itself a money-capture maneuver by the biggest clubs. What England did *right* was keep the pyramid connected: pro/rel with the EFL survived the breakaway. What it did *wrong*, hollowing out revenue-sharing, creating the parachute-payment distortion, and presiding over 50+ lower-league insolvencies since 1992, is precisely what a designed-from-scratch American pyramid could avoid. The Women's Super League's 2018 licensing-based professionalization is the closest real-world analog to the transition mechanism a rebuilt pyramid would need.

The central finding: **there is no legal or financial mechanism to dissolve the MLS/NWSL franchise model against its owners' will** (a federal jury upheld the USSF/MLS structure against antitrust challenge in *NASL v. USSF*, decided February 2025). The viable paths are (a) making an open pyramid more profitable than a closed one so incumbents opt in, (b) regulatory pressure through USSF sanctioning standards, and (c) letting USL's open system compete MLS into convergence. All three are developed in Part VI.

## Part I: The US Professional Landscape

### 1.1 The sanctioning system (why "Division 1" is a license, not a table position)

Unlike anywhere else in world football, US division status is not earned on the pitch. The US Soccer Federation (USSF) sanctions leagues into Divisions I–III via its **Professional League Standards (PLS)**, with annual applications. Key Division I men's requirements:

| Requirement | D1 Men | D1 Women | D2 Men | D3 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Minimum teams | 12 (14 by year 3) | 8 (10 by year 4) | 8 | 8 men / 6 women |
| Time zones | ET, CT, PT | 2 (3 by year 6) | ET + CT or PT | none |
| Market size | 75% in 1M+ metros | 75% in 750K+ | 75% in 750K+ | none |
| Stadium capacity | 15,000 | 5,000 | 5,000 | 1,000 |
| Principal owner net worth | $40M (35% stake) | $15M | $20M | $10M |
| Ownership group net worth | $70M combined | $25M | — | — |
| Performance bond | $1M | $100K | — | $250K / $20K |

The PLS is the single most powerful lever in American soccer that nobody elected. It was designed to prevent league collapses, a rational response to the NASL's 1984 death and two failed women's leagues, but it functionally hard-codes wealth requirements into the sport's structure and makes small-market, community-owned clubs structurally ineligible for the top flight regardless of sporting merit. Any restructuring plan ultimately runs through the USSF board and these standards. The standards survived their biggest legal test: a jury in *North American Soccer League v. USSF* (E.D.N.Y., verdict February 2025) found the divisional system did not violate antitrust law.

### 1.2 Major League Soccer (Division I men, 1996– )

**Structure.** MLS is a **single-entity LLC**. Club "owners" are technically investor-operators holding equity in the league itself, and player contracts are held centrally by the league, not the clubs. This structure was designed explicitly to defeat antitrust challenges to salary restraints, and it worked: the structure survived *Fraser v. MLS* (1st Cir. 2002), where the court treated MLS as a single entity but declined to rule the question definitively, disposing of the case on the players' failure to prove a relevant market. Two consequences matter most.

- **There is no independent club to "sell back to fans."** An MLS club is a revocable operating right plus a share of a Delaware LLC. Dissolving the franchise model means restructuring the LLC itself, a unanimous-consent-grade corporate event, not a league rule change.
- Salary controls (a salary budget in the mid-single-digit millions per club, with Designated Player slots, allocation money mechanisms, and U22 initiative slots layered on top) are league policy, not collectively bargained caps in the NFL sense, though the CBA with the MLSPA governs the framework. Roster mechanisms like allocation money and discovery rights are notoriously opaque, itself a fan-alienation cost.

**Franchise fee escalation** (the "parked money" curve):

| Club | Year | Expansion fee |
|---|---|---|
| Chicago Fire | 1998 | $5M |
| Toronto FC | 2007 | $10M |
| Seattle/Philly/Portland/Vancouver/Montreal | 2009–12 | $30–40M |
| NYCFC | 2015 | $100M |
| FC Cincinnati / Nashville | 2019–20 | $150M |
| St. Louis City | 2023 (awarded 2019) | $200M |
| Charlotte FC | 2022 (awarded 2019) | $325M |
| **San Diego FC** | **2025 (awarded 2023)** | **$500M** |

MLS reached 30 teams in 2025 and has stated no near-term plans to expand further. Sportico reported at the San Diego announcement that MLS held 18 of the world's 50 most valuable soccer franchises, more than the Premier League's 9, despite not being a top-10 league on sporting quality. That gap between franchise valuation and sporting output is the empirical core of the "safe space to park money" thesis. Valuations are driven by scarcity (closed membership), real estate and stadium plays, and the league's growth narrative, not by competitive success, which carries no downside risk (no relegation) and limited upside (capped spending).

**The November 2025 calendar decision.** MLS's Board of Governors voted on November 13, 2025 to move to a summer-to-spring calendar: a 14-game "sprint season" February–May 2027, then the first full 2027–28 season starting July 2027 with a midwinter break and MLS Cup in May 2028. Stated rationale: transfer-window alignment, FIFA calendar alignment, playoff decongestion. Reporting also indicates a shift toward a single-table format divided into geographic divisions is under discussion, though not finalized, and that changing *spending rules* is proving a harder internal sell than the calendar. The read: MLS is voluntarily converging toward global norms where convergence is costless to the franchise model (calendar, formats) while defending the parts that protect asset values (closed membership, cost controls). That marks exactly where the negotiation boundary sits.

**Media.** The Apple TV deal (10-year, ~$250M/yr structure signed 2022) was restructured for 2026: MLS Season Pass as a separate paywall was eliminated and matches moved inside the general Apple TV subscription, a tacit admission that the hard-paywall strategy suppressed reach.

### 1.3 NWSL (Division I women, 2013– )

**History matters here.** NWSL is the *third* attempt at a top women's league. WUSA (2001–03) and WPS (played 2009–11, folded 2012) both collapsed. That trauma explains the league's conservative early economics and USSF's initial subsidization of national-team player salaries.

**Current state (2026):** 16 teams after Boston Legacy and Denver Summit joined. The expansion fee curve mirrors MLS: Bay FC and Boston at $53M (2023), then **Denver at $110M (January 2025)**, the largest fee in US women's sports history. Angel City's controlling-stake sale (2024) at a **$250M valuation** made it the world's most valuable women's team. The 2024–2027 media deal (CBS, ESPN, Amazon, Scripps) is worth $240M over four years, roughly 40x the prior deal.

**The 2024 CBA is genuinely radical by US standards, and it matters enormously here:**
- **First major US league to abolish the draft entirely** (college, expansion, all of it)
- Unrestricted free agency for all players at contract expiry; guaranteed contracts; no trades without consent
- Salary cap: $3.3M (2025) rising to $5.1M (2030), plus revenue-sharing top-ups; minimum salary $48.5K (2025) rising to $82.5K (2030)
- Explicit framing from Commissioner Berman: NWSL competes in a *global* labor market, so it aligned with global standards

**Friction point:** in December 2025 the league unilaterally introduced a "High Impact Player" rule allowing up to $1M in over-cap spending on stars (Trinity Rodman's ~$2M+ deal being the headline outcome), and the NWSLPA filed a grievance in January 2026 alleging the unilateral change violates labor law. It is the same "flexibility vs. cost control" tension MLS resolved with the DP rule in 2007, and worth watching.

NWSL has already done, voluntarily, half of what open reform asks: it abandoned the draft and movement restrictions to match the global game, because the WSL was beating it for talent. Competition from an open system forced convergence. That is the theory of change, demonstrated in the women's game before anyone tried it in the men's.

### 1.4 The USL system: the live open-pyramid experiment

- **USL Championship** (D2, men): ~24 clubs, independent ownership (not single-entity), no salary cap, no academy territorial restrictions.
- **USL League One** (D3, men); **USL League Two** (pre-professional); **USL Super League** (women's, sanctioned Division I in February 2024, running a fall–spring calendar from launch).
- **USL Premier** (announced February 2025; name unveiled January 2026): a new men's Division I league launching **2028** as a single national table with a long-term target of 20 clubs (12–14 at launch; ~8 promoted from the Championship plus 4–6 expansion sides expected). In March 2025, USL owners voted by supermajority to implement **promotion and relegation** across all three men's tiers, the first US pro league ever to do so, with initial reporting suggesting two up/two down. USL hired Tony Scholes, formerly the Premier League's chief football officer, in late 2025 to run the new division.
- **Constraint to watch:** the 15,000-seat D1 stadium requirement. Only a couple of current USL venues comply; Pittsburgh (5,500 to 15,000, ~$125M project), Sacramento, Detroit, Raleigh and others have stadium projects racing the 2028 deadline. The USSF's PLS is thus simultaneously the enabler and the chokepoint of the open-pyramid experiment.

USL's bet is that *jeopardy is a product*. If USL Premier launches successfully and pro/rel demonstrably drives engagement and club investment in mid-size markets (Louisville, Sacramento, Indianapolis, Phoenix), the "American exceptionalism" argument against open systems dies on contact with evidence. If it stumbles on stadium economics or relegation-driven insolvencies, it hands MLS a decade of "told you so." The whole case for reform is heavily leveraged to this one experiment.

### 1.5 MLS NEXT Pro and the reserve layer

MLS NEXT Pro (D3, 2022–) is primarily MLS reserve sides plus a few independents, a development league rather than a community-club tier. It is discussing whether to follow MLS's calendar flip. Its existence means MLS already controls a two-tier structure internally, a closed shadow of a pyramid.

## Part II: The Youth Landscape (through U19)

### 2.1 The league map (post-Development Academy)

US Soccer shuttered its Development Academy in 2020 (COVID being the trigger, financial strain the cause), fragmenting elite youth soccer into competing private platforms:

| Platform | Gender | Governance | Cost profile |
|---|---|---|---|
| **MLS NEXT** | Boys | Major League Soccer | Free at MLS-club academies ("Homegrown Division," ~29 club academies); $3,000+ base fees at non-MLS "Academy Division" clubs |
| **ECNL / ECNL-RL** | Boys & Girls | Private (club-controlled) | $3,000–$8,000 dues; $8,000–$15,000+ all-in |
| **Girls Academy (GA)** | Girls | Private | Comparable to ECNL |
| **USYS National League / NPL / EDP / ECRL** | Both | US Youth Soccer / private | $2,500–$7,000 mid-tier |
| **Recreational (AYSO, parks)** | Both | Community | $100–$400/yr |

### 2.2 The cost cliff

Aggregating current reporting: recreational soccer costs $125–$400/year; competitive club soccer $2,500–$5,000 in fees plus $1,000–$3,000 in travel, gear, and tournaments; elite national-league soccer routinely $8,000–$15,000, with some families reporting up to ~$25,000/year. A full youth career at the elite tier can exceed $100,000 per child. The cliff appears at ages 10–12, exactly when serious development begins. Hidden costs (mandatory uniform packages, "optional" showcases, coach per-diems) mean advertised fees routinely understate true cost by 50–100%.

A ~$100K household income is roughly the de facto gate for the travel tier, and the structure is regressive twice over: families pay directly, *and* the system's geographic sprawl (national showcases, cross-country league travel) converts income into access even among those who can nominally afford the fees.

### 2.3 Why it stays broken: the missing money loop

In most of the world, youth development is financed by clubs because developed players are *assets*. FIFA's Regulations on the Status and Transfer of Players mandate **training compensation** (paid to clubs that trained a player ages 12–21 when he signs his first pro contract abroad or transfers internationally through age 23) and the **solidarity mechanism** (5% of any international transfer fee distributed to clubs that trained the player ages 12–23).

The US broke this loop:
- USSF declared "neutrality" in 2015 and refuses to enforce these mechanisms domestically, citing antitrust fear.
- MLS only began *complying* with FIFA's international rules in April 2019, after the Crossfire Premier/DeAndre Yedlin dispute exposed the gap, and the MLSPA formally opposes the mechanisms as player-movement restraints.
- Critically, **RSTP does not apply to domestic transfers**, so a youth club that develops a player who signs for an MLS side directly typically receives nothing. Note too that RSTP training compensation (Art. 20) does not apply to women's football at all, so any money-loop fix for the women's pyramid needs a purpose-built mechanism, not a copy of the men's rules. Development produces no revenue; therefore parents are the revenue.

England's youth system (EPPP, 2012–) is imperfect and criticized on other grounds, but academy players do not pay. Clubs fund academies because the compensation architecture makes players recoverable assets. Any US youth fix that does not create a domestic money loop (domestic training compensation, or pyramid clubs with transfer-fee upside) is a scholarship band-aid on a structural wound. One sign of movement: cooperative and community-ownership youth models are emerging as grassroots counter-programming (Solstice FC, for example, runs a constitutionally fee-capped cooperative). They are small today, but ideologically aligned with an open, affordable pyramid.

### 2.4 Geography

Regionalization is well-supported by the evidence: national footprints at U13–U19 are a cost multiplier with negligible development benefit (the Netherlands and England develop world-class players inside travel radii smaller than many US *metro areas*). England's step 3+ men's leagues and the FA Women's National League split North/South precisely to cap travel. A properly dense US pyramid would regionalize below the second or third tier for adults, and almost entirely for youth.

## Part III: England, MLS/NWSL vs. EFL/WSL, Read Honestly

### 3.1 The Football League and the 1992 breakaway

- The Football League (1888) grew to four divisions with pooled TV money: pre-1992, television revenue split roughly 50% to Division One, 25% to Division Two, and 12.5% each to the lower two. Solidarity was structural.
- In October 1990, LWT's Greg Dyke convened the "Big Five" (Man Utd, Liverpool, Arsenal, Everton, Spurs) to plan a breakaway that would concentrate TV money on the top flight. The FA, feuding with the Football League, blessed it (*Blueprint for the Future of Football*, June 1991). The Founder Members Agreement was signed July 17, 1991, and the First Division resigned from the Football League on February 20, 1992.
- Sky won the rights with a £304M five-year bid, and the money went to the 22 (soon 20) breakaway clubs alone. The domestic deal is now £6.7B per cycle.
- **The one thing the breakaway did not break: promotion and relegation.** Three up, three down with the Football League survived, and 51 different clubs have since played in the Premier League. This is the load-bearing fact for the whole argument. England's top flight is, functionally, *a commercial breakaway that retained sporting openness*: proof that concentrated commercial value and an open pyramid can coexist, and that jeopardy became part of the product Sky sold.

### 3.2 The costs England paid (a checklist of what to avoid)

1. **The revenue chasm.** Cutting the lower divisions out of the TV pool contributed to 50+ insolvency events in the EFL since 1992.
2. **Parachute payments** (introduced 2006–07, now ~£225–233M/yr to recently relegated clubs over up to three seasons, ~£40M+ in year one) versus solidarity payments of roughly £5M per non-parachute Championship club and under £1M lower down. Academic work (Wilson et al.; Birkbeck/Liverpool briefings) finds parachute clubs are roughly twice as likely to be promoted, measurably reducing Championship competitive balance, a "revolving door" between the divisions.
3. **Wage inflation as arms race.** Championship clubs chasing promotion have routinely run wage-to-revenue ratios near or above 100%. The relegation "cliff edge" cuts revenue ~73% (parachutes limit it to ~42%) while wages fall only ~28% in year one.
4. **Regulatory whack-a-mole.** Profitability & Sustainability Rules (PSR) produce points deductions after the fact (Leicester City docked 6 points in early 2026 for exceeding its £83M three-year assessed loss threshold; West Brom docked 2), rather than preventing distress. England has now legislated an **Independent Football Regulator** (Football Governance Act 2025) with power to impose distribution settlements between the PL and EFL. Put plainly, the state had to intervene because the leagues could not agree on solidarity. EFL chair Rick Parry's counter-proposal: abolish parachutes, pool 25% of broadcast revenue across all four divisions on merit.

**Design lesson:** any American pro/rel system has to write the redistribution architecture (revenue-pooling percentages, squad-cost ratios rather than after-the-fact loss limits, relegation wage-adjustment clauses in standard player contracts) into the founding documents. The one advantage of building late is building it in rather than bolting it on.

### 3.3 The WSL: the closest analog to the transition mechanism reform needs

The WSL's history is a top-down restructure that *worked*, and it maps almost one-to-one onto the mechanism a US transition would need:

- **2011:** FA launches WSL with 8 teams selected **by application** (16 applied), a licensed, closed launch. No relegation for two seasons.
- **2014:** WSL 2 created; pro/rel introduced *within* the WSL.
- **2016:** promotion from the wider pyramid into WSL 2 opened, and the licensed league was **stitched back into the open pyramid**.
- **2018–19:** full professionalization by **re-licensing**: every club had to re-apply against criteria (minimum 16-hour player contracts, mandatory academies). Sunderland was denied a license and dropped despite on-field results; Man United (re-formed 2018) and others entered by application. It was controversial and litigated in the court of public opinion, but it worked: the WSL became fully professional in seven years and became the global standard-setter that forced NWSL's CBA revolution.
- **2023–25:** governance spun out of the FA into a club-owned company (NewCo, then Women's Professional Leagues Ltd, now WSL Football); second tier rebranded WSL 2; top flight expanding to 14 for 2026–27.

The WSL demonstrates the exact hybrid an American restructure would use: *licensing as the bridge between a closed launch and an open pyramid*. Incumbents get security during the capital-intensive build phase, and sporting merit takes over once the tier below is viable. It also shows the failure mode to guard against: 11 of 12 WSL clubs are appendages of men's Premier League clubs, so "openness" in practice still routes through incumbent wealth. An American women's pyramid that wants independent clubs (the Angel City and Kansas City Current model) to thrive has to protect against becoming an MLS-affiliate farm system.

### 3.4 Also note: England is the outlier, not the baseline

Germany's 50+1 fan-ownership rule, licensing systems in the Bundesliga and elsewhere, and membership-owned giants (Barcelona, Real Madrid, Bayern) show multiple viable ownership architectures inside open pyramids. "Responsible ownership plus winning pedigree" as a financial-incentive design has more working precedents than just England.

## Part III-B: The Rest of the Big Five (and Their Women's Leagues)

*Added in v0.2. England is covered in Part III; this section covers Spain, Germany, Italy, and France, each read for what it contributes to the mission's design space, especially ownership architecture.*

### 3B.1 Spain: La Liga & Liga F

**Ownership: the accidental mixed-model proof.** Spain's 1990 Sports Law forced clubs to convert into *Sociedades Anónimas Deportivas* (SADs, sports corporations) as a debt-crisis response, but exempted the four clubs that were then solvent: **Real Madrid, Barcelona, Athletic Club, and Osasuna remain member-owned non-profits (socios models) competing in the same league as privately-owned corporations.** The conversion obligation itself was eliminated in 2022, but the four exempt clubs had already held their status for three decades by then. Thirty-five years of evidence make the point: the mixed model is not merely viable, and the two member-owned giants are the league's most valuable brands. This is the single most direct precedent for a league with teams funded in different ways.

**Financial regulation:** La Liga runs the strictest *ex ante* cost control in Europe. Each club receives a computed squad-cost limit (*límite salarial*) before the season, enforced by blocking player registrations rather than punishing after the fact (the mechanism behind Barcelona's registration crises). This "you cannot spend it in the first place" design is superior to England's after-the-fact PSR points deductions, and is the model a new US pyramid should copy.

**The private-equity cautionary tale:** La Liga's 2021 "Impulso" deal sold ~8.2% of media revenue to CVC Capital Partners for ~€2B over **50 years**, and Real Madrid, Barcelona, and Athletic refused to participate and litigated. Contrast that with Germany's outcome below: two federations faced the same PE offer, and the fan-owned one walked away.

**Liga F (women):** became **Spain's first fully professional women's league in 2021–22** by government (CSD) designation, run as an independent league body. Professionalization was immediately followed by labor conflict, the 2023 players' strike over minimum salaries, months before the World Cup win and the Rubiales scandal forced a broader reckoning. The lesson: professionalization on paper without floor-wage enforcement reproduces the pre-professional exploitation with better branding. Barcelona Femení, a *socios*-owned club, is the sport's global standard-bearer, repeatedly filling Camp Nou with 90,000+. Member ownership and women's-game excellence are demonstrably compatible.

### 3B.2 Germany: Bundesliga & Frauen-Bundesliga

**The 50+1 rule** (DFL statute since 1998): to be licensed for the Bundesliga or 2. Bundesliga, the parent members' association must hold 50% of voting rights plus one share in the professional football company. Grandfathered exceptions exist for 20-year continuous benefactors (Bayer Leverkusen, Wolfsburg; Hoffenheim's Hopp exception was voluntarily reversed in November 2024), and RB Leipzig complies technically while restricting membership, the known exploit. In June 2025 the Federal Cartel Office concluded its long review by ruling that further exceptions will no longer be tolerated, and the Chancellor publicly endorsed the rule in September 2025. Effects attributed to it: Europe's highest attendances (~42,000+ average), €20 standing tickets, low insolvency rates, and fierce fan-culture continuity.

**The February 2024 DFL investor collapse is the decade's most important ownership-politics event.** Clubs voted in December 2023 to sell 8% of a new media-rights company to private equity (~€1B, 20 years; CVC and Blackstone the finalists). Organized fan protests (tennis-ball barrages, remote-control cars on pitches, bike-locked goals, matches delayed to near-abandonment) plus a legitimacy crisis over Hannover 96's contested vote forced the DFL to kill the deal within ten weeks. **Fans defeated institutional private equity in open field.** For the argument that financial incentives should reward responsible ownership rather than PE, Germany supplies both the constitutional mechanism (50+1) and the proof that fan power can enforce it.

**Frauen-Bundesliga:** the counterweight caution. Founded 1990 (two regional divisions, merged 1997), federation-run (DFB), expanded to 14 teams in 2025–26, but still effectively **semi-professional**, with women's-only clubs historically unable to survive without corporate backing and average attendance an order of magnitude below the WSL's leaders. Germany proves that fan-ownership doctrine alone does not professionalize a women's league. Dedicated investment and independent league governance (which England and Spain built) are separate, necessary work. The DFB is now under pressure to fund a standalone women's-league push.

### 3B.3 Italy: Serie A & Serie A Femminile

**Ownership:** the opposite pole from Germany, near-total private ownership with the heaviest foreign (especially US institutional) presence in Europe: RedBird (Milan), Oaktree (Inter, via loan default seizure from Suning), Friedkin (Roma), Pagliuca (ex-Atalanta), and others. Italy is the live experiment in exactly what a cost-control-free system produces: distressed-asset and leveraged ownership at scale, chronic stadium underinvestment (most clubs rent municipal grounds), and a league whose competitive brand has eroded relative to its 1990s peak despite on-pitch quality. **Italy is the "what MLS owners might become without cost controls" scenario, not a model.**

**Serie A Femminile:** founded 1968 (Europe's oldest of the five), taken over by the federation (FIGC) in 2018, and made **professional in 2022–23, the first professional women's sports league in Italian history**, driven top-down by the FIGC after the 2019 World Cup surge. Nearly every club is an appendage of a men's Serie A side (Juventus dominant), and all current top-flight women's clubs were founded or absorbed in the last two decades, the fastest and most complete example of the "men's clubs buy up the women's pyramid" capture pattern flagged in §3.3. In 2026, Serie A Women was among 14 women's leagues formally admitted into the European Leagues association, a continent-wide governance milestone worth tracking.

### 3B.4 France: Ligue 1 & Première Ligue

**The media-rights fragility lesson.** France's football economy has been structurally destabilized twice in five years by broadcast-partner failures: the Mediapro collapse (2020, mid-contract) and the DAZN dispute and exit (2024–25), forcing the league to launch its own direct-to-consumer channel (Ligue 1+) in 2025. Combined with DNCG financial oversight (France's long-standing independent club-finance auditor, itself a good regulatory model) and PSG's state-linked dominance, France demonstrates that **a league whose economics hang on a single volatile media deal is fragile regardless of pyramid structure**, directly relevant to MLS's single-buyer Apple dependence (§1.2, §6.3).

**Première Ligue (women):** formerly Division 1 Féminine, restructured in 2024 under a new professional league body (Ligue Féminine de Football Professionnel) with the Arkema-sponsored "Première Ligue" rebrand, France following the England/Spain playbook of spinning women's football out of pure federation control into a dedicated professional league entity. Lyon's dynasty built the modern women's club game, and PSG money followed. As in Italy and England, top-club capture by men's institutions is near-total.

### 3B.5 Synthesis: Designing a Mixed-Ownership League (the regulatory core of the plan)

Across the Big Five plus Brazil (Part V-B), every ownership architecture a US restructure needs already exists, *and coexists with the others inside single leagues*:

| Model | Working example | What it contributes to a US design |
|---|---|---|
| Member/supporter-owned (socios, e.V.) | Real Madrid, Barcelona, Athletic, Osasuna; all German 50+1 clubs | Loyalty moat, price discipline, anti-PE bulwark |
| Fan-majority hybrid (50+1) | Bundesliga statute | Investment capital *with* member veto, the compromise instrument |
| Private/family corporate | Most of Serie A, Ligue 1, EFL | Speed of capital, but requires fit-and-proper tests plus cost controls |
| Benefactor/industrial | Bayer Leverkusen, Wolfsburg | Grandfathering template for legacy stakeholders |
| League-equity investor-operator | MLS single-entity | The incumbent US model, convertible per §6.2 |
| Club-corporation with association parent | Brazil SAF (Part V-B) | The statutory conversion mechanism the US lacks |

**Design principles the evidence supports for a unified US pyramid:**
1. **Ownership-form pluralism as explicit policy.** Spain proves member-owned and corporate clubs compete on equal footing for decades, so a US pyramid should *license the club, regulate the behavior, and stay agnostic on the ownership form.* The claim that supporter-owned clubs build differentiated fan loyalty is borne out by Bundesliga attendance and pricing data, and by St. Pauli-style identity clubs.
2. **Protect the form, don't mandate it.** A US 50+1 mandate is politically impossible against incumbent capital, but *protections for clubs that choose member ownership* (golden shares, supporter right-of-first-refusal on sale or relocation, name/crest/color heritage locks, now UK regulator territory too) are achievable and create the "choice architecture" for fan ownership to grow organically.
3. ***Ex ante* cost control (La Liga model) over *ex post* punishment (England model).** Blocked registrations prevent the harm; points deductions merely memorialize it.
4. **Independent financial auditor (DNCG model)** rather than league-committee self-policing.
5. **Media-revenue concentration limits.** No single buyer above a set share of rights, and no PE sale of league media equity without a supermajority *of clubs and a supporter-representation mechanism*, which is the German fans' February 2024 victory, codified.

## Part IV: The UEFA Champions League's Role (the layer a "USSF Champions League" would emulate)

What the UCL actually does for Europe:

1. **It converts domestic league position into a continental jackpot**, making every national league's top-four (or top-N) race meaningful. Qualification is the incentive engine of 55 domestic leagues simultaneously.
2. **Scale of money (2024/25, first season of the new 36-team format):** total men's club competition revenue €4.4B; €3.4B distributed to clubs. Each league-phase club receives a guaranteed ~€18.6M, plus performance pay (~€2.1M/win) and a value pillar, and deep runs are worth well north of €100M (Inter's 2024/25 run brought in ~€137M).
3. **It funds solidarity:** UEFA commits 10% of gross competition revenue to solidarity, €308M in 2024/25 to clubs *not* participating in the league phases (up 76% year-on-year), routed to youth development. This is the redistribution layer the closed US model lacks entirely.
4. **Its dark side is instructive:** UCL money is the primary driver of domestic competitive imbalance across Europe. The same clubs qualify, compound their advantage, and (as the 2021 Super League attempt showed) eventually try to close the system from the top. Any US continental layer needs distribution caps or it will recreate the parked-money dynamic one level up.

**Applicability:** the US analog isn't purely hypothetical. CONCACAF Champions Cup already exists (MLS, Liga MX, and others qualify), and the Leagues Cup is a nascent US–Mexico layer. A "USSF Champions League" is better understood as a *domestic inter-divisional and inter-regional* competition, closer in function to an FA Cup and UCL hybrid, that gives lower-pyramid clubs revenue events and gives regionalized divisions a national narrative. The US Open Cup (1914–, the oldest ongoing US soccer competition) is the neglected asset here: it already provides open, all-pyramid competition, and MLS has repeatedly tried to deprioritize it. Reviving and monetizing the Open Cup is the cheapest possible pilot of the whole thesis.

## Part IV-B: The Lamar Hunt U.S. Open Cup, Full Analysis

*Added in v0.2. Expands the "cheapest possible pilot" recommendation from Part IV into a standalone assessment.*

### 4B.1 What it is and how it works

The Open Cup is the oldest ongoing national soccer competition in the United States, first contested 1913–14 as the **National Challenge Cup** (Brooklyn Field Club the first winner), played for the Dewar Challenge Trophy, one of the oldest nationally contested trophies in American team sport. It was consciously modeled on England's FA Cup: **open to every affiliated team in the country, amateur or professional**, single elimination. It was renamed for Lamar Hunt in 1999.

Current mechanics: roughly 100 clubs from MLS, USL Championship, USL League One, MLS Next Pro, NISA, and the amateur "Open Division" (which, per a July 2025 USSF change, now enters entirely through qualifying rounds rather than league-allocated tracks). Lower divisions enter in early rounds; MLS enters late (in 2025, 16 MLS teams entered at the fourth round, with entry allocation coordinated against Champions Cup and Leagues Cup commitments). The winner earns a CONCACAF Champions Cup berth. A 2016 rule bars teams majority-owned by a higher-tier club, the anti-reserve-team firewall that made the 2023 MLS Next Pro gambit (below) a rule change request rather than a default right.

### 4B.2 History in four eras

1. **Industrial/immigrant era (1914–1950s):** the Cup *was* American soccer's national championship. Bethlehem Steel (5 titles, 1915–26) and Fall River Marksmen (5 titles by 1931) were company- and mill-town powerhouses, and the final was a home-and-away series from 1928–1968. This era also contains the foundational cautionary tale: in **1928 the American Soccer League barred its clubs from the Cup ("the Soccer War")** in a power struggle with the federation and FIFA. The conflict helped collapse the ASL and bury American soccer for two generations. The 2023–24 MLS standoff is a direct historical rhyme, and the USSF knows it.
2. **Ethnic-club era (1950s–1980s):** German-American, Greek-American, Italian-American and Maccabee clubs kept the Cup alive as the pro game withered; Maccabee LA won five titles (1973–81); Greek American AA's three-peat (1967–69) still stands. The **NASL (1967–84) almost entirely ignored the Cup**, the first top-flight league to treat it as beneath itself, an omission that contributed to the NASL having no roots when its TV money vanished.
3. **MLS era (1996–2022):** MLS entered from its first season, restoring a true national championship. All US-based MLS teams received automatic berths from 2012 (64-team field, later ~100). MLS sides have won every edition since **Rochester Rhinos (USL) in 1999**, the last lower-division winner, the fact that defines both the Cup's problem (MLS dominance) and its romance (the 27-year wait for the next giant-killing). Sacramento Republic (USL Championship) reaching the **2022 final** is the modern high-water mark for the pyramid's underdogs.
4. **Crisis and rescue era (2023– ):** below.

### 4B.3 Finances and incentives

- **Prize money, historic:** through 2025, $300,000 to the champion, $100,000 runner-up, $25,000 to the furthest-advancing team from each lower division. Trivial against MLS budgets, transformative for amateur and D3 sides.
- **Prize money, current:** the **2026 edition doubled the pot** to $600,000 champion, $250,000 runner-up, $50,000 per-division awards, plus the newly created John Motta Trophy for the deepest-advancing Open Division side. Directionally right, still roughly two orders of magnitude below FA Cup distributions.
- **The real economics are hosting economics.** For USL and amateur clubs, a home draw against an MLS side is often the biggest gate of the season. USL leadership stated plainly during the 2024 crisis that these matches are "some of the biggest games of the season... especially in terms of revenue." Meanwhile, MLS clubs privately argued they *lose money* on Cup participation (midweek fixtures, small away venues, no broadcast return). **This asymmetry is the whole political economy of the Cup:** its value flows down the pyramid while its costs fall on the top, exactly what a closed-league operator has no reason to fund and an open pyramid would treat as core solidarity infrastructure.
- **Broadcast:** historically the Cup's chronic failure, with no coherent central rights package, matches scattered across streams, and no marketing budget comparable to Leagues Cup (which MLS co-owns with Liga MX and placed on its own Apple platform). The competition's underexposure is a choice, not a market verdict.

### 4B.4 The 2023–24 war, blow by blow (because it maps the power structure)

1. **May 2023:** Commissioner Garber publicly calls the Cup "a very poor reflection on what it is that we're trying to do with soccer at the highest level."
2. **December 15, 2023:** MLS Board of Governors votes to withdraw first teams from the 2024 Cup, proposing MLS Next Pro (D3 reserve) teams instead. Framing: fixture congestion and player development. Critics note the four-year-old Leagues Cup adds more fixtures than the Cup ever did, and that the PLS arguably *requires* D1 participation in federation competitions.
3. **December 20, 2023:** USSF's Pro League Taskforce **denies** the waiver. Public backlash is universal, with Independent Supporters Council condemnation and USL siding openly with fans ("We stand with fans across the country who want to see it remain an authentic and inclusive competition").
4. **January–February 2024:** private USSF–MLS negotiations reopen what fans thought settled. Open Cup Committee chair Duncan Mattson, told to "stand down" from brokering a rules-based resolution, **resigns in protest**, saying he "would never allow MLS to pick and choose the rules and policies that they follow."
5. **March 1, 2024:** compromise, with only **8 MLS senior teams** entering 2024; 11 MLS Next Pro sides fill in; CONCACAF-participating clubs sit out entirely.
6. **2025:** partial restoration, with 16 MLS teams entering; defending champion LAFC becomes the first modern-era holder not to defend (Champions Cup priority); prize doubling announced for 2026; Nashville SC wins the 2025 final over Austin FC, its first-ever trophy, marketed heavily by the club itself, a small proof that MLS clubs *can* value the Cup when they win it.

**What the war proved:** (a) the USSF will bend to MLS in private even after standing firm in public, so federation independence (§6.3's "governance capture" risk) is real and quantified; (b) organized public opinion moved the outcome, and the backlash bought the Cup its survival and, plausibly, the 2026 prize doubling; (c) MLS's objection is economic, not principled, which means it is purchasable.

### 4B.5 Attempts to make it an FA Cup: failed and successful

**Failed or stalled:**
- *Prize scaling to relevance.* Even doubled, $600K cannot move an MLS roster decision; the FA Cup's pull rests on nine figures of distributed value plus a Wembley final.
- *Broadcast packaging.* Repeated USSF efforts never produced a marquee rights deal; the Cup has no equivalent of the BBC/ITV free-to-air showcase that makes FA Cup upsets national events.
- *Scheduling dignity.* Midweek graveyard slots, finals hosted at the finalist's stadium rather than a neutral cathedral, and constant calendar subordination to league, Leagues Cup, and CONCACAF play.
- *Reserve-team dilution* (2023). Attempted and beaten back, but the attempt itself did brand damage the FA Cup has never suffered.

**Successful or promising:**
- *Surviving 1928 and 2023.* Institutional persistence is itself the asset; 100+ consecutive editions minus only the COVID years (2020–21) is a heritage moat no new competition can buy.
- *The 2016 reserve-team ban.* Protected competitive authenticity a decade before it was tested.
- *The upset economy.* Sacramento 2022, the annual amateur "cupset" cycle, and a devoted independent media ecosystem (TheCup.us has covered it for decades) prove latent demand.
- *2025–26 reforms.* Prize doubling, the Motta Trophy, cleaner Open Division qualifying, and coordinated MLS entry allocation: the first coherent USSF investment package in the Cup's modern history.
- *The USL alignment.* With USL Premier launching in 2028, the Cup becomes the venue where the two rival D1s can actually meet. **A USL Premier side beating MLS opposition in a well-broadcast Cup run is the single highest-leverage narrative event available to the open-pyramid movement.** The Cup is where the league war becomes legible to casual fans.

**Reform package this brief recommends (for the roadmap in Part VII):** a ring-fenced free-to-air broadcast deal; a permanent neutral-venue final rotated among iconic stadiums; a prize fund tied to a fixed percentage of USSF commercial revenue (self-scaling); guaranteed lower-seed hosting through the Round of 32; and a solidarity kicker, where every Cup gate involving a top-two-division club shares a fixed percentage with the lower-division participant's academy.

## Part V: The Geographic Thesis, Stress-Tested

The scale argument, with numbers attached:

| Entity | Area (km²) | Population | Top-flight pro clubs (men) |
|---|---|---|---|
| England | ~130,300 | ~57M | 20 (PL) + 72 EFL + ~1,600 pyramid clubs |
| New York State | ~141,300 | ~19.5M | ~2 (MLS) |
| Contiguous US | ~8,080,000 | ~340M (US total) | 30 MLS across 4 time zones |
| Europe (continent) | ~10,180,000 | ~745M | ~700+ top-flight clubs across 55 associations |
| EU alone | ~4,230,000 | ~450M | — |

The directional claim holds: **the US is continent-scaled but league-structured like a single mid-sized country.** England fits inside New York State and supports 92 fully professional clubs plus thousands below; the entire US supports roughly 90–100 professional men's clubs (MLS + MLS NEXT Pro + USL system) across a landmass 60x larger.

Two honest caveats that sharpen rather than weaken the argument:

1. **Population density, not just landmass, is the binding constraint.** Europe has 2.2x the US population on similar land. The right US comparison set is "state = country": California (~39M) sits near Poland or Spain, Texas (~31M) above Australia, Florida (~23M) near the Netherlands and Belgium combined. Each of those European comparators sustains a full professional pyramid with pro/rel. On a per-capita basis the US pro-club deficit is roughly 5–8x versus Western Europe, enormous, but the "each state as a footballing country" framing should be pitched on population, where it is bulletproof, rather than area alone.
2. **Travel economics genuinely do break the naive pyramid.** A national US third division is a flight league; a national English third division is a bus league. This is why going divisional below tier ~2 is not a compromise but a requirement, and why USL Premier's choice of a *single national table* at D1 is rational (the top tier can afford flights) while regional splits belong at D3/D4 and throughout youth. Mexico (similar geography-compression problems) and Brazil (state championships feeding national Séries A–D) are the working precedents for regionalized lower tiers under national upper tiers on continental landmasses. Brazil is arguably the single best structural comparator for the US, and the next section is a dedicated deep-dive.

## Part V-B: Brazil, The Continental-Scale Case Study

*Added in v0.2. Brazil is the only footballing nation that shares the United States' defining constraint, continental geography, and it has solved (and mis-solved) nearly every problem in this brief.*

### 5B.1 The dual-pyramid architecture

Brazil runs **two simultaneous, independent pyramid systems**, and clubs compete in both every year:

- **The national pyramid (CBF-run):** Série A, B, and C (20 clubs each) and Série D (40 clubs), connected by promotion/relegation (typically four up/four down), running roughly April/May–December.
- **The state pyramids (27 state federations):** every state runs its own multi-tier championship (São Paulo's Paulistão has four levels; small states may have one or two), played January–April in the big-club states, with their own promotion/relegation. Flamengo can be winning Série A nationally while a club is being relegated within its own state league. The two systems don't touch except at defined gateways.

**The gateways are the genius of the design:**
1. **Série D entry:** the best-placed state championship clubs not already in Séries A–C qualify for Série D, meaning *any club in any town in Brazil has a defined, annual, merit-based path into the national professional system.* Clubs founded from nothing have climbed to Série A within years (São Caetano, founded 1989, was a Série A runner-up in 2000; Brasiliense, founded 2000, reached Série A in 2005).
2. **Copa do Brasil entry:** state championship placement also feeds Brazil's FA Cup analog, which pays meaningful prize money down the pyramid and offers a Copa Libertadores berth. The state leagues thus function as the qualifying membrane between community football and national and continental competition.

**Translation to the US:** Brazil is the existence proof for the Part V design, *state-level competition as the base layer, national divisions on top, with automatic annual qualification gateways between them.* The US already has 50+ state associations under USSF and adult amateur state cups. What's missing is the gateway (a Série D equivalent that state champions qualify into) and the incentive (Open Cup berths plus prize money flowing to state-level winners, which Part IV-B's reforms would supply). The National Premier Soccer League and USL League Two are proto-Série-D material.

### 5B.2 The SAF law: Brazil's answer to the ownership-conversion problem

Until 2021, Brazilian clubs were legally **non-profit civil associations**: tax-exempt, member-governed, and unsellable, which produced a century of community rootedness and, eventually, catastrophic debt and governance dysfunction at giants like Botafogo, Cruzeiro, and Vasco. **Law 14.193/2021 created the *Sociedade Anônima do Futebol* (SAF)**, a purpose-built football corporation form. The key mechanics are all relevant to a US conversion design:

- **Three formation routes:** transform the association wholesale; spin off the football operations into a new SAF while the association survives as parent and shareholder; or found an SAF from scratch. The SAF inherits the club's competition rights, contracts, and identity.
- **Debt architecture:** a centralized judicial execution regime pools the old club's creditors, and the SAF must dedicate 20% of revenues to servicing legacy obligations. Investors buy the future without the past ambushing them, and creditors get an orderly queue.
- **Capital instruments:** SAFs can issue regulated securities including dedicated football debentures (*debentures-fut*), opening capital-markets funding beyond a single benefactor.
- **Mandatory governance:** permanent boards of directors and fiscal councils with statutory election and removal rules, a governance floor the old association model lacked.

**Results after four operating years (through 2025):** roughly a third of Série A has converted or attracted investment. Botafogo (John Textor/Eagle Football; Libertadores champions 2024), Cruzeiro (Ronaldo, then resold), Bahia (City Football Group), Atlético-MG, and Fortaleza (explicitly modeling a Bayern-style structure) all converted, while **Vasco's 777 Partners deal collapsed with control returned to the association by court order in 2024**, the PE failure case, on schedule. Crucially, **SAFs, traditional associations (Flamengo and Palmeiras, the two financially strongest clubs, remain member-owned associations by choice), and pre-existing corporations (Red Bull Bragantino) all compete in the same league.** Ownership pluralism, statutorily enabled, competitively neutral. Meanwhile Série A's average operating revenue grew 73% from 2015–2024 to €69.3M, now sixth globally, ahead of the EFL Championship, Eredivisie, and Primeira Liga, on the back of World Cup-legacy stadiums, betting sponsorship, and player-trading income.

**Translation to the US:** the SAF law is the statutory template for §6.2's "equity conversion" pathway. It demonstrates that a legislature can create a *voluntary, incentive-laden corporate form* that moves clubs between ownership architectures without expropriating anyone, while ring-fencing legacy obligations. An American analog (call it a Community Club Conversion framework) could run in the opposite direction from Brazil's: giving investor-owned franchises a tax- and governance-advantaged path to *partial member ownership*, and giving new pyramid clubs a standard form with supporter golden shares baked in.

### 5B.3 Brazil's warnings

1. **Calendar cannibalism:** playing state, national, cup, and continental competitions yields 70+ match seasons for big clubs, and the state championships that democratize access also exhaust the elite. A US design should cap the base layer's claim on top-division squads (or, per Open Cup practice, stagger entry rounds).
2. **Vestigial-institution drag:** state federations are century-old political fiefdoms, and their championships persist partly because the officials they sustain vote in CBF governance. Federated structures accrete politics, so design sunset and review clauses.
3. **Corporate form does not equal competence:** Football Benchmark's four-year SAF review is blunt that legal reform brought capital but not automatically discipline, and volatility and short-termism survived the conversion. Textor's Botafogo won the Libertadores and then imploded managerially within months. Ownership architecture is necessary, not sufficient.
4. **Export-economy trap:** Brazilian club economics lean on selling players to Europe, sustainable for Brazil because its talent supply is bottomless, but it caps league quality. A US pyramid that funds itself by exporting every good player to Europe reproduces the current national-team ceiling. Domestic wage growth, the thing pro/rel-driven investment races produce, is the counterweight.

## Part VI: BA Assessment of the Plan

### 6.1 Restating the plan

1. Dissolve the franchise model at D1 (MLS, NWSL)
2. Unify the ladder under single men's and women's brands with tiered naming (MLS to MLS Championship to MLS League 1; women's equivalent)
3. Go regional below ~tier 2; regionalize youth aggressively
4. Rebuild financial incentives around responsible ownership and sporting merit rather than VC/PE asset appreciation
5. Add a domestic champions-league layer
6. Compensate incumbent franchise buyers ("money talks")

### 6.2 What the evidence says about each element

**Dissolution (element 1), reframed.** There is no lever to dissolve MLS involuntarily. The antitrust route just failed (*NASL v. USSF*, jury verdict for the federation and MLS, Feb 2025); Congress isn't touching it; USSF revoking MLS's D1 sanction is legally possible but politically unthinkable and would be litigated for a decade. The realistic version of element 1 is **convergence under competitive and regulatory pressure**, with three concrete mechanisms:

- **The USL flank (market pressure).** If USL Premier plus pro/rel works, MLS faces a rival selling jeopardy, community authenticity, and open access, the exact product differentiation MLS can't copy without restructuring. NWSL's draft abolition under WSL pressure is the proven template: closed leagues liberalize when an open competitor starts winning the narrative and the talent.
- **The PLS lever (regulatory pressure).** USSF could amend Professional League Standards over a 10–15 year horizon to require pyramid interconnection, minimum solidarity distributions, or academy-access standards as conditions of D1 sanctioning, grandfathering incumbents on a schedule. Post-*NASL* verdict, USSF's discretion over standards is legally fortified; the barrier is board politics (and MLS's structural influence within USSF), not law.
- **The licensing bridge (the WSL mechanism).** A future unification event, say an MLS/USL accommodation in the 2030s, could use WSL-style licensing: incumbents keep guaranteed top-flight status for N years (their fee treated as having purchased that license period), while pro/rel operates beneath and phases upward. Closed launch, then stitched pyramid, then merit takeover. It's the only transition mechanism with a real-world success record.

**Compensation (element 6), the actual math.** Thirty MLS clubs at an average valuation plausibly in the $600–700M+ range implies $18–21B of paper equity to protect, against perhaps $3.5–4B of historically paid-in expansion fees. No refund is possible; the money has to *convert*. Options, in rough order of viability:

1. **Equity conversion:** franchise stakes convert into shares of a unified league media and commercial entity (the thing that actually generates the value). Owners keep the asset, and the sporting layer opens underneath. Precedent: the Premier League itself is a company whose shareholders are its current member clubs, where relegated clubs surrender their share to the promoted club, and English club values survived fine.
2. **Time-limited immunity licenses:** for example, 10 seasons of guaranteed D1 status post-unification, tradeable, decaying. This prices in the fee as a term purchase.
3. **Relegation insurance plus parachute design:** guaranteed multi-year revenue floors for relegated founding members, funded by the larger unified media pool, England's parachute concept, but capped and sunset to avoid England's distortion.
4. **Fan and community buyback provisions:** right-of-first-refusal for supporter trusts at relegation or sale, Germany-style golden-share protections. Politically potent, financially marginal to incumbents, so pair it with options 1 to 3 rather than leading with it.

The pitch to owners has to be EPL-shaped: *the open pyramid is worth more.* More clubs, more markets, more matches of consequence, promotion races and relegation battles as premium inventory. The Premier League's founders broke away to get richer and accidentally proved jeopardy sells; the American unification case is "get richer on purpose."

**Unified branding (element 2), two flags.** First, MLS-as-masterbrand assumes MLS wins the coming decade; if USL Premier succeeds, the better-branded league may not be MLS, so the naming architecture should stay league-agnostic. Second, on the women's side, a "WMLS" would subordinate what is currently the *stronger* global brand: NWSL out-earns and out-values nearly every women's league on earth and just set the global standard on player freedom. The women's pyramid (NWSL plus USL Super League, which is already D1-sanctioned and fall–spring) needs *interconnection*, not renaming, and any unification should treat NWSL as the brand asset, not the target.

**Regionalization (element 3), the strongest element, essentially unopposed by evidence.** Costed correctly, regional D3/D4 and hyper-regional youth attack the pay-to-play problem at its cost driver (travel) rather than its symptom (fees). Pair it with §2.3's money loop: domestic training compensation plus transfer fees give regional clubs a business model beyond parent invoices. This is also the element achievable *without* anyone's permission, since state associations, USL League Two, NPSL, and community clubs can densify now.

**Financial incentive redesign (element 4).** Concrete instruments, drawn from the evidence base: squad-cost ratios (UEFA's new model, spend as a share of revenue, rather than England's after-the-fact loss limits); pooled broadcast revenue with a fixed pyramid share written into founding documents (Parry's 25% proposal is the reference number); relegation wage-adjustment clauses standardized in player contracts (which kills the cliff edge); ownership fit-and-proper tests plus multi-club-ownership limits; and a solidarity levy on all transfer fees. None of this is speculative, since every instrument exists somewhere in Europe today.

**Domestic champions layer (element 5).** The cheapest pilot is to rehabilitate the US Open Cup: guarantee MLS full-squad participation via sanctioning conditions, put real prize money behind it, and market the giant-killing. Then a Leagues-Cup-style regional interdivisional competition. Build the UCL analog only once the pyramid exists to feed it, and cap its distribution share from day one (Part IV's lesson).

### 6.3 Risk register (top items)

| Risk | Severity | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| USL Premier undercapitalized / stadium deadlines slip | High | The open-pyramid proof-of-concept failing sets the thesis back 10+ years |
| Pro/rel-driven insolvencies in early years | High | England's 50+ insolvencies; mitigation must be in founding docs (squad-cost ratios, parachutes with sunsets) |
| MLS/Apple content economics entrench closure | Med | A single-buyer media model has no per-market incentive to add jeopardy |
| MLSPA/NWSLPA opposition to training compensation | Med | Unions view it as a player-movement tax; youth-funding design must find a player-neutral mechanism |
| USSF governance capture | Med | The PLS lever only works if USSF acts independently of D1 incumbents |
| Women's game collateral damage | Med | Unification designs drafted around the men's game historically strip-mine the women's (see WSL's PL-affiliate capture) |

### 6.4 Where the history gives clues, condensed

1. **1992 England:** commercial concentration and an open pyramid can coexist, and jeopardy is monetizable, but write redistribution into the constitution or the state eventually writes it for you (Football Governance Act 2025).
2. **2018 WSL:** licensing is the working bridge from closed to open. Expect a Sunderland (an incumbent that loses out and screams), and survive it.
3. **2019–24 NWSL:** open-system competition forces closed-system liberalization faster than any lawsuit.
4. **2025 NASL v. USSF:** the courts will not open the pyramid for anyone.
5. **2025–28 USL:** the experiment is running, and the near-term job is to help it succeed (attendance, coverage, capital, advocacy), because every subsequent argument depends on its results.
6. **2026 World Cup:** a home tournament, record goals, a group win, a knockout win, and the same Ro16 ceiling. The talent pipeline, not the national-team program, is the binding constraint. The pipeline is Parts II and V.

## Part VII: Timeline, Plausibility Assessment, 2026–2056

*Added in v0.2. Probability estimates below are analytical judgments, not data. Treat them as structured priors for planning, to be revised as events land.*

### 7.1 The hosting clock (the mission's outer deadline)

2030 is Spain/Portugal/Morocco (with centennial matches in South America); 2034 is Saudi Arabia. Under FIFA's confederation-rotation norms, CONCACAF's earliest realistic re-entry is 2038, but the competitive bid landscape (UEFA re-entry pressure, an expected 2038/2042 Asian and European carve-up, and FIFA's preference for spacing regions) makes **2046 or 2050 the modal window for a North American men's World Cup return**, roughly 20–24 years out, comfortably inside a 30-year horizon and consistent with this brief's ~2056 framing. The plan therefore needs the *pyramid functioning by the late 2030s* to have a full development generation (a 2040 six-year-old is a 2056 22-year-old) pass through it.

### 7.2 Phase 0: The Proof Window (2026–2030)

**What happens on the current trajectory (no advocacy required):** MLS sprint season (Feb–May 2027) and calendar flip (July 2027 onward); USL Premier launch 2028 with pro/rel across three tiers; NWSL cap escalation and HIP-rule labor resolution; the Open Cup's doubled prize fund beds in; the 2028 LA Olympics keeps soccer investment warm.

**What the mission needs from this phase:** USL Premier launching with at least 12 compliant stadiums and surviving its first relegations without an insolvency; at least one USL-vs-MLS Open Cup moment that breaks into mainstream coverage; the women's pyramid question (NWSL and USL Super League interconnection) at least being *asked* publicly.

**Plausibility that Phase 0 lands adequately: ~60–70%.** The stadium chokepoint is the main hazard; the USL's hiring of Premier League operational leadership and owners' capital commitments are the main reassurance.

### 7.3 Phase 1: Competitive Pressure (2028–2034)

The two-D1 era. Expected dynamics, from the precedents in this brief: MLS responds to USL Premier the way it responded to every prior competitive stimulus, with selective convergence (calendar already done; likely next, spending liberalization, possibly Open Cup re-commitment) while defending closed membership. NWSL and USL Super League women's dynamics run the same play at faster clock speed, because the women's game already proved (2024 CBA) it converges under pressure. The Open Cup becomes the annual referendum, where each cross-league result is narrative ammunition.

**Key markers to watch:** USL Premier attendance and media numbers versus the USL Championship baseline; the first pro/rel "miracle club" story (a Louisville, Sacramento, or Detroit deep run); whether MLS's 2027–28 format flirtation with a single table plus divisions extends toward *any* sporting consequence for finishing last; USSF board composition and whether PLS review opens.

**Plausibility that by ~2034 an open pyramid is demonstrably viable in the US market: ~50%.** A coin-flip, resolved mostly by Phase 0.

### 7.4 Phase 2: The Unification Window (2033–2041)

This is where the central question lands: **is structural transformation plausible within 10–15 years?** Assessment: *the full plan (single unified branded ladder, franchise model dissolved) is unlikely on that clock (~10–15%). A functionally open pyramid is genuinely plausible (~40–50%),* via one of three routes, in ascending order of ambition:

1. **Parallel-pyramid equilibrium (most likely single outcome):** MLS remains closed at the top; USL runs the open pyramid beside it; the Open Cup and CONCACAF places arbitrate between them; youth regionalizes under both. This is not the full plan, but it delivers perhaps 60% of the developmental benefit, because the *pyramid's existence*, not MLS's participation in it, is what multiplies professional clubs and free academies.
2. **Convergence by acquisition or accommodation:** an MLS–USL structural accommodation in the mid-2030s (interleague play, then Cup integration, then pro/rel between the MLS Next Pro tier and USL tiers, then eventually conditional access to MLS itself), likely triggered by a media-rights renegotiation (the post-Apple deal in the early 2030s is the natural forcing event). This is where §6.2's equity-conversion and licensing-bridge mechanisms activate. The WSL precedent says the licensing bridge takes ~7 years from decision to full merit-based operation, so a decision by ~2033 completes by ~2040.
3. **Regulatory forcing:** post-2026-cycle USSF leadership (federation elections, World Cup-surplus funding independence) amends the PLS to require pyramid interconnection for D1 sanctioning on a 10-year grandfathered schedule. Legally fortified post-*NASL*, but politically the longest shot, unless routes 1 and 2 have already shifted owner incentives, in which case regulation ratifies rather than forces.

**Youth and Open Cup tracks run faster than the league track** and shouldn't wait for it: domestic training-compensation reform, state-gateway competitions (the Série D analog), and Open Cup reform are all achievable inside 10 years because they don't require incumbent owners to surrender anything material.

### 7.5 Phase 3: Maturation (2041–2056)

If a functionally open pyramid exists by ~2040, the maturation phase needs: two full pro/rel cycles without systemic insolvency (validating the financial architecture of §3B.5 and §3.2); club count growth from ~100 to a 300–500 professional and semi-pro band (roughly Argentine, not English, density, the realistic per-capita target); free-to-play elite youth as the norm within pyramid-club academies, with pay-to-play surviving only as a genuine recreational tier; and the women's pyramid reaching the same state *on its own brand equity* (the NBA/WNBA lesson applied in reverse: the NWSL name, not a derivative). A 2042–2046 window where US clubs regularly matter in an expanded CONCACAF and intercontinental club landscape is the external validation marker.

**Composite plausibility of the mission's end-state, the US recognized as a legitimate footballing nation, with an open, dense, largely free-to-develop pyramid, by a ~2050 World Cup return: ~35–45%,** with the single largest swing factor being Phase 0's USL Premier launch, and the second largest being whether the youth money loop (training compensation plus gateway competitions) gets built in the next decade regardless of what the D1 leagues do.

**The strategic corollary:** the controllable near-term work is not persuading MLS. It is (a) making USL Premier succeed, (b) reforming the Open Cup and youth funding loop through USSF channels, and (c) building the intellectual and public case (documents like this one) so that when the forcing events arrive (the post-Apple media negotiation, the post-World Cup USSF election cycle, the first USL giant-killing), the open-pyramid position has a fully drafted, financially literate plan waiting.
